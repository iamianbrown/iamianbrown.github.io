AIAA Liquid Rocket Initiative

Providing students with hands-on experience designing, building, and testing liquid rocket engines.

Our Mission

The AIAA Liquid Rocket Initiative was founded to create a place at UIUC where students would gain hands-on experience designing, building, and testing liquid rocket technology.

This is important as the number of talented rocket engineers is one of the primary constraints on the development rate of interplanetary transport technology.

Grunt: Our First Engine

Grunt is an 1100 lbf thrust chamber assembly that will be tested at Purdue's Zucrow Labs this fall. Its primary function is to serve as a proof of concept and to provide a basis for further iteration in the future. In order to get a test article on the stand as quickly as possible, we opted to not use a cooling system in our combustion chamber, and to run a 400 ms "burp" test instead, significantly simplifying our design. 

Grunt is in the final stages of manufacturing, with our test currently planned for mid-September.

Our Design Methodology

When designing a rocket, there is an infinite number of potential issues that could occur. This can suck the designer into a rabbit holes of possible concerns, making forward progress impossible. To avoid this dilemma, we ask two questions: "what problem are we trying to solve?" and "what is the minimum set of actions, based on our current knowledge, needed to solve that problem?" 

We use these questions to drive an iterative design procedure where we make a simple design, then use feedback on that design to refine the answers to the questions. This allows us to get the big picture right before we set in on the details.

Future Projects

Test Stand

Now that Grunt is almost complete, we have begun to design our own test stand. Building our own test stand will allow us to decrease our iteration times and it will give us experience with many of the control systems needed to build a flight vehicle.

MK II

Since Grunt is only designed for a 400 millisecond burn time, it will not be suitable for a flight vehicle. To address this need we have begun work on a new engine, MK II. MK II will use ablative cooling to keep the engine walls cool, allowing for an eight secondd burn time and a flight to 30,000 feet.
